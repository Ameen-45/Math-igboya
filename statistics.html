Introduction to Statistics

Statistics is the science of collecting, analyzing, interpreting, presenting, and organizing data. It enables us to make informed decisions in the face of uncertainty. It plays a crucial role in various fields, such as economics, social sciences, medicine, and engineering, by allowing us to draw meaningful conclusions from data.

By understanding and applying statistical methods, one can assess risks, forecast trends, and even uncover hidden patterns within large datasets. Whether predicting stock market behavior, analyzing medical test results, or studying population demographics, statistics provides the tools to make data-driven decisions.

Historical Context

Modern statistics originated in the 18th century with work by mathematicians like Carl Friedrich Gauss and Pierre-Simon Laplace, who developed key methods for analyzing data. In the early 20th century, the contributions of Ronald Fisher, Karl Pearson, and others helped establish foundational statistical techniques still used today.

As the demand for analyzing complex data grew in fields like agriculture, economics, and social sciences, statistics continued to evolve. Today, it plays an essential role in data science, machine learning, and artificial intelligence.

Key Concepts

Descriptive

Descriptive statistics is the process of summarizing and describing data in a meaningful way. It includes measures such as the mean, median, mode, variance, and standard deviation, which help to understand the central tendency and spread of the data.

Visualizations like bar charts, histograms, and pie charts are commonly used to present descriptive statistics, making the data easier to interpret.

Inferential

Inferential statistics is used to draw conclusions about a population based on a sample. Techniques like hypothesis testing, confidence intervals, and regression analysis allow statisticians to make predictions and assess the likelihood of certain outcomes.

Inferential methods help quantify uncertainty, giving a level of confidence in the findings drawn from a sample rather than the entire population.

Probability

Probability is a mathematical framework that quantifies uncertainty. It provides a way to model random events and predict the likelihood of their occurrence based on known information.

Common probability concepts include random variables, probability distributions (e.g., binomial, normal distribution), and conditional probability, all of which are essential for building models in statistics.

Bayesian

Bayesian statistics is a probabilistic approach that updates the probability for a hypothesis based on new evidence or data. Unlike frequentist statistics, which relies on fixed data, Bayesian methods incorporate prior knowledge and adjust beliefs as new information becomes available.

This approach is particularly useful in dynamic environments where knowledge is continually updated, such as in real-time data analysis or machine learning applications.

Data Types & Collection

Qualitative (Categorical)

Qualitative data, also known as categorical data, refers to non-numeric data that can be used to categorize or label variables. It describes qualities or characteristics of the subjects being measured.

  • Non-numerical descriptions
  • Examples: Colors, brands, yes/no responses, types of fruits
  • Subtypes:
    • Nominal (no order: categories without any intrinsic ranking, such as male/female, types of fruits, or zip codes)
    • Ordinal (ordered: categories with a defined order, such as survey ratings, education levels, or rankings)

Qualitative data is often used in categorical analysis to group similar observations together and identify patterns within different categories.

Quantitative (Numerical)

Quantitative data represents measurable quantities that can be expressed numerically. This type of data is often used for statistical analysis where the aim is to measure the magnitude or amount of something.

  • Measurable numerical values
  • Examples: Height, temperature, sales, income, age
  • Subtypes:
    • Discrete (countable: data that can only take certain distinct values, such as the number of students in a class, cars in a parking lot, or employees in an office)
    • Continuous (measurable: data that can take any value within a range, such as height, weight, or time)

Quantitative data allows for more detailed and precise analysis, including various statistical operations such as mean, median, and standard deviation.

Collection Methods

The way data is collected can significantly affect the quality and accuracy of the statistical analysis. Here are the most common methods:

  • Surveys: Questionnaires designed to gather responses from a sample group. Useful for collecting data on opinions, behaviors, or preferences.
  • Experiments: Controlled tests that manipulate one or more variables to determine their effect on other variables.
  • Observational: Data is collected by observing and recording behavior or phenomena without intervening or manipulating the environment.
  • Secondary: Using existing datasets collected for other purposes. These can be obtained from sources like government databases, research studies, or online platforms.

Measures of Dispersion

Range

Max - Min

Simple measure of spread

Example: Range of 5, 10, 15, 20, 25 is 25 - 5 = 20

Another Example: Range of 3, 7, 9, 5, 10 is 10 - 3 = 7

Variance

σ² = Σ(x - μ)² / N

Average squared deviation from the mean

Example: Variance of dataset [5, 10, 15, 20, 25] is 50

Another Example: Variance of dataset [3, 5, 7, 8] is 3.25

Standard Deviation

σ = √σ²

In original units, tells how spread out the data is

Example: Standard Deviation of dataset [5, 10, 15, 20, 25] is approximately 7.07

Another Example: Standard Deviation of dataset [3, 5, 7, 8] is approximately 1.80

IQR (Interquartile Range)

Q3 - Q1 (middle 50%)

Robust to outliers, shows the range of the middle 50% of the data

Example: IQR of [3, 5, 7, 8, 10, 12] is Q3 (10) - Q1 (5) = 5

Another Example: IQR of [1, 2, 4, 5, 6, 8, 10, 12] is Q3 (8) - Q1 (3.5) = 4.5

Calculating Standard Deviation

Dataset: [5, 10, 15, 20, 25]

  1. Mean (μ) = 15
  2. Deviations: -10, -5, 0, 5, 10
  3. Squared deviations: 100, 25, 0, 25, 100
  4. Variance = (100 + 25 + 0 + 25 + 100) / 5 = 50
  5. Standard Deviation = √50 ≈ 7.07

Probability

Basic Rules

P(E) = (# favorable) / (# possible)

  • Addition: P(A∪B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A∩B)
  • Multiplication: P(A∩B) = P(A) × P(B|A)
  • Complement: P(A') = 1 - P(A)

Example 1: If P(A) = 0.5 and P(B) = 0.4, and A and B are mutually exclusive, P(A∪B) = 0.5 + 0.4 = 0.9

Example 2: If P(A) = 0.6 and P(B|A) = 0.5, then P(A∩B) = 0.6 × 0.5 = 0.3

Example 3: If P(A) = 0.3, then P(A') = 1 - 0.3 = 0.7

Distributions

  • Discrete: Binomial, Poisson
  • Continuous: Normal, Uniform
  • Parameters: Mean, variance, shape

Example 1: In a binomial distribution with n = 5 and p = 0.4, P(X = 2) can be calculated using the binomial formula.

Example 2: For a normal distribution, the probability of a value falling within one standard deviation of the mean is approximately 68%.

Example 3: For a uniform distribution, every value within a specified range has the same probability of occurring.

Conditional Probability

P(A|B) = P(A∩B) / P(B)

Probability of A given B has occurred.

Example 1: If P(A∩B) = 0.2 and P(B) = 0.4, then P(A|B) = 0.2 / 0.4 = 0.5

Example 2: If a deck of cards is shuffled, the probability of drawing a king (A) given that the card drawn is a face card (B) is P(A|B) = 4/12 = 1/3

Dice Probability Example

P(rolling 4 on die) = 1/6 ≈ 16.67%

P(rolling even number) = 3/6 = 0.5

P(rolling ≥5) = 2/6 ≈ 33.33%

Example: If you roll two dice, the probability of getting a sum of 7 is P(sum = 7) = 6/36 = 0.1667

Example: P(rolling a double 6 on two dice) = 1/36 ≈ 2.78%

Statistical Distributions

Normal Distribution

  • Bell-shaped curve
  • Symmetrical about mean
  • 68-95-99.7 rule
  • Parameters: μ (mean), σ (std dev)

Example 1: In a normal distribution with μ = 0 and σ = 1, about 68% of the values fall within the range of -1 to 1.

Example 2: In a height distribution of adult women with μ = 160 cm and σ = 10 cm, approximately 95% of women will have a height between 140 cm and 180 cm.

Binomial Distribution

  • Fixed number of trials (n)
  • Independent trials
  • Constant success probability (p)

Example 1: In a coin toss experiment (n = 10 tosses, p = 0.5 for heads), the binomial distribution can be used to calculate the probability of getting exactly 4 heads.

Example 2: If you are testing a drug's effectiveness and have 20 trials with a success rate of 0.7, the binomial distribution can calculate the probability of having exactly 15 successful treatments.

Poisson Distribution

  • Counts rare events
  • Fixed time/space interval
  • Parameter: λ (rate)

Example 1: If the average number of cars passing a checkpoint in an hour is 10, the Poisson distribution can be used to find the probability of exactly 8 cars passing in a given hour.

Example 2: If a call center receives an average of 2 calls per minute, you can use the Poisson distribution to determine the probability of receiving exactly 5 calls in the next minute.

Normal Distribution Properties

  • ≈68% within 1σ of μ
  • ≈95% within 2σ of μ
  • ≈99.7% within 3σ of μ
  • Z-scores standardize values

Statistical Inference

Confidence Intervals

Range of plausible values for a parameter

CI = x̄ ± z*(σ/√n)

Example 1: A sample mean of 50 with a standard deviation of 5 and a sample size of 25 gives a 95% confidence interval for the population mean of [48.0, 52.0].

Example 2: A 99% confidence interval for the average time spent in a store (x̄ = 10 minutes, σ = 2 minutes, n = 100) would be calculated as [9.61, 10.39] minutes.

Hypothesis Testing

  1. State null (H₀) and alternative (H₁)
  2. Choose significance level (α)
  3. Calculate test statistic
  4. Compare to critical value
  5. Make decision (reject/fail to reject H₀)

Example 1: In a study to determine if a drug affects blood pressure, the null hypothesis might be H₀: "The drug has no effect," and the alternative hypothesis would be H₁: "The drug affects blood pressure." The p-value is then compared to α = 0.05 to decide whether to reject or fail to reject H₀.

Example 2: A factory produces lightbulbs with a lifetime of 1200 hours. Testing if the lifetime differs from 1200 hours could involve calculating the test statistic and comparing it to the critical value to make a decision.

Common Tests

  • t-test: Compare means
  • χ² test: Test independence
  • ANOVA: Compare >2 means
  • Regression: Model relationships

Example 1: A t-test can be used to determine if the average height of two groups of students differs significantly.

Example 2: A χ² test might be used to determine if there is an association between gender and voting preference.

Example 3: ANOVA is used when comparing the mean scores of three or more different groups, such as testing the effect of different teaching methods on student performance.

Example 4: Regression analysis could model the relationship between advertising expenditure and sales volume.

Correlation & Regression

Correlation

r = Σ[(x - x̄)(y - ȳ)] / [(n - 1)sₓsᵧ]

  • Measures linear relationship (-1 to 1)
  • Does not imply causation
  • r > 0: Positive correlation
  • r < 0: Negative correlation
  • r = 0: No linear relationship

Example 1: A correlation coefficient of r = 0.8 indicates a strong positive linear relationship between two variables (e.g., study hours and exam scores).

Example 2: A correlation coefficient of r = -0.6 indicates a moderate negative linear relationship between two variables (e.g., hours of TV watched and exam scores).

Linear Regression

ŷ = b₀ + b₁x

  • Predicts dependent variable (ŷ)
  • b₁ = slope, b₀ = y-intercept
  • R² measures goodness of fit (0 to 1)
  • R² = 1: Perfect fit; R² = 0: No fit

Example 1: A linear regression equation ŷ = 10 + 3x suggests that for every unit increase in x, the predicted value of y increases by 3.

Example 2: A high R² value (e.g., R² = 0.9) indicates that the model explains 90% of the variability in the dependent variable.

Study Time vs Exam Score

Hypothetical regression equation:

Score = 50 + 5 × (Study Hours)

Interpretation: Each additional hour of study predicts a 5-point increase in the exam score.

Example: If a student studies for 4 hours, the predicted exam score would be:

Score = 50 + 5 × 4 = 70

Thus, the student is expected to score 70 on the exam.

Real-World Example: A/B Testing

Website wants to test if a new design (B) has a higher conversion rate than the original (A):

  1. Randomly assign users to A or B
  2. Collect conversion data (e.g., 5% for A, 7% for B)
  3. Perform hypothesis test (e.g., two-proportion z-test)
  4. If p-value < 0.05, conclude B performs significantly better
