Calculus

Introduction to Calculus

Calculus is a branch of mathematics focused on rates of change and accumulation of quantities. It provides powerful tools for modeling and analyzing dynamic systems in mathematics, science, and engineering.

Don't be intimidated! Calculus might seem challenging at first, but it's incredibly rewarding once you grasp the fundamental concepts.

Historical Context

Developed independently by Isaac Newton and Gottfried Leibniz in the late 17th century. Ancient precursors include Archimedes' method of exhaustion and Indian mathematics of the Kerala school.

Key Branches

Differential Calculus

Study of rates of change and slopes of curves

Example: Finding instantaneous velocity

Integral Calculus

Study of accumulation and areas under curves

Example: Calculating work done by a force

Example: Fluid flow analysis

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

Connects differential and integral calculus:

∫ab f'(x) dx = f(b) - f(a)

This remarkable theorem shows that differentiation and integration are inverse processes.

Limits and Continuity

Limits are the foundation of calculus! Mastering this concept will make everything else much easier to understand.

Definition of Limit

The limit of f(x) as x approaches a is L if we can make f(x) arbitrarily close to L by taking x sufficiently close to a.

limx→a f(x) = L

Example: limx→2 (3x+1) = 7

Limit Laws

  • Sum Law: lim(f+g) = lim f + lim g
  • Product Law: lim(f·g) = lim f · lim g
  • Quotient Law: lim(f/g) = lim f / lim g (if lim g ≠ 0)
  • Power Law: lim(f)n = (lim f)n

These laws allow us to break complex limits into simpler parts.

Continuity

A function f is continuous at a point a if:

  1. f(a) is defined
  2. limx→a f(x) exists
  3. limx→a f(x) = f(a)

Polynomials are continuous everywhere.

Important Special Limits

limx→0 (sin x)/x = 1

limx→∞ (1 + 1/x)x = e

limx→0 (ex - 1)/x = 1

Derivatives

Derivatives unlock the power to understand change! This concept has countless applications in physics, economics, and beyond.

Definition

The derivative measures instantaneous rate of change:

f'(x) = limh→0 [f(x+h) - f(x)] / h

Geometrically, this represents the slope of the tangent line.

Common Derivatives

  • d/dx (xn) = nxn-1
  • d/dx (sin x) = cos x
  • d/dx (cos x) = -sin x
  • d/dx (ex) = ex
  • d/dx (ln x) = 1/x

These are the building blocks for more complex derivatives.

Differentiation Rules

  • Sum Rule: (f+g)' = f' + g'
  • Product Rule: (fg)' = f'g + fg'
  • Quotient Rule: (f/g)' = (f'g - fg')/g2
  • Chain Rule: (f(g(x)))' = f'(g(x))·g'(x)

The chain rule is especially powerful for composite functions.

Applications of Derivatives

This is where calculus comes alive! These applications show how derivatives solve real-world problems across many fields.

Rates of Change

  • Velocity as derivative of position: v(t) = ds/dt
  • Acceleration as derivative of velocity: a(t) = dv/dt
  • Marginal cost in economics: C'(x) ≈ cost of producing one more unit
  • Population growth rates in biology

If s(t) = t³ - 4t² + 5t, then v(t) = 3t² - 8t + 5

Optimization

  • Finding maxima and minima using critical points
  • First derivative test for local extrema
  • Second derivative test for concavity
  • Applied to business (profit maximization) and engineering (material efficiency)

To maximize a box's volume V = x(10-2x)², find dV/dx = 0

Curve Analysis

  • Increasing when f'(x) > 0, decreasing when f'(x) < 0
  • Concave up when f''(x) > 0, concave down when f''(x) < 0
  • Inflection points where concavity changes
  • Asymptotic behavior and end behavior analysis

Related Rates

  • Problems involving multiple changing quantities connected by equations
  • Applications in physics (ladder problems) and geometry (expanding circles)
  • Requires implicit differentiation
  • Four-step solution process

If a 5m ladder slides down at 0.5m/s, how fast does the base move when top is 3m high?

Integrals

Integration is like mathematical detective work - finding the original function from its rate of change. You've got this!

Definition

The integral represents the accumulation of quantities and the area under a curve.

∫ f(x) dx = F(x) + C

where F'(x) = f(x)

Riemann sums: limn→∞ Σ f(xi*)Δx

Common Integrals

  • ∫ xn dx = xn+1/(n+1) + C (n ≠ -1)
  • ∫ sin x dx = -cos x + C
  • ∫ cos x dx = sin x + C
  • ∫ ex dx = ex + C
  • ∫ 1/x dx = ln|x| + C
  • ∫ sec²x dx = tan x + C

Integration Techniques

  • Substitution: Reverse chain rule
  • Integration by parts: ∫ u dv = uv - ∫ v du
  • Partial fractions: Decompose rational functions
  • Trigonometric integrals: Powers of sin/cos
  • Trig substitution: For √(a²±x²) forms

Applications of Integrals

Integrals help us measure and calculate things in our 3D world - from the volume of your coffee cup to the force of a waterfall!

Area Calculation

  • Area between curves: ∫ (top - bottom) dx
  • Polar area: ½∫ r² dθ
  • Parametric area: ∫ y(t)x'(t) dt
  • Area using horizontal vs. vertical elements

Volume

  • Disk method: π∫ [f(x)]² dx
  • Washer method: π∫ (R² - r²) dx
  • Shell method: 2π∫ (radius)(height) dx
  • Cross-sections: ∫ A(x) dx

Volume of sphere: π∫-rr (r² - x²) dx = 4/3πr³

Physics

  • Work: W = ∫ F(x) dx
  • Center of mass: (∫ xρ(x)dx)/mass
  • Fluid pressure: P = ρgd
  • Arc length: ∫ √(1 + (dy/dx)²) dx
  • Surface area of revolution

Probability

  • Probability density functions: ∫-∞∞ f(x)dx = 1
  • Expected value: E[X] = ∫ xf(x)dx
  • Normal distributions: (1/σ√2π)e-(x-μ)²/2σ²
  • Cumulative distribution functions

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

This is the crown jewel of calculus! The FTC connects the two main concepts in a beautiful way.

First Fundamental Theorem

Connects differentiation and integration:

∫ab f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a)

where F is any antiderivative of f

∫13 2x dx = 3² - 1² = 8

Second Fundamental Theorem

Shows that differentiation reverses integration:

d/dx ∫ax f(t) dt = f(x)

For a ≤ x ≤ b

Explains why antiderivatives work for evaluation

FTC Applications

  • Evaluating definite integrals without Riemann sums
  • Finding average value of functions: (1/(b-a))∫ab f(x)dx
  • Net change theorem: ∫ rate of change = net change
  • Accumulation functions

Advanced Techniques

These powerful techniques will help you solve integrals that seem impossible at first glance!

Integration by Parts

∫ u dv = uv - ∫ v du

Useful for products of functions (polynomial × exponential, etc.)

∫ x ex dx = x ex - ex + C

Trigonometric Substitution

  • √(a²-x²) → x = a sinθ
  • √(x²+a²) → x = a tanθ
  • √(x²-a²) → x = a secθ

∫ dx/√(4-x²) → x = 2sinθ → ∫ dθ = θ + C = sin⁻¹(x/2) + C

Partial Fractions

Decomposes rational functions into simpler fractions

For P(x)/Q(x) where deg(P) < deg(Q)

1/[(x+1)(x-2)] = A/(x+1) + B/(x-2)

Improper Integrals

  • Type 1: Infinite limits
  • Type 2: Discontinuous integrands
  • Convergence tests

∫1∞ 1/x² dx = limb→∞ [-1/x]1b = 1

Infinite Series

Series let us work with infinity in finite ways - they're the bridge to advanced mathematics!

Convergence Tests

  • Comparison Test: Compare to known series
  • Ratio Test: lim |an+1/an| < 1
  • Root Test: lim (an)1/n < 1
  • Integral Test: Compare to ∫ f(x)dx
  • Alternating Series Test: For (-1)n series

Power Series

∑n=0∞ cn(x-a)n

Radius of convergence R = 1/lim sup |cn|1/n

Geometric series: ∑ xn = 1/(1-x) for |x| < 1

Taylor Series

f(x) = ∑n=0∞ [f(n)(a)/n!](x-a)n

Maclaurin series when a = 0

ex = ∑ xn/n! = 1 + x + x²/2! + x³/3! + ...

Series Applications

  • Approximating functions (sin, cos, exp, ln)
  • Solving differential equations
  • Evaluating non-elementary integrals
  • Numerical analysis and computations
  • Fourier series for periodic functions

Applications
