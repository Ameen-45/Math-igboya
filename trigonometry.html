Trigonometry

Trigonometry is a branch of mathematics that studies relationships between the angles and sides of triangles. Derived from Greek words "trigonon" (triangle) and "metron" (measure), it is fundamental in STEM fields and beyond.

Historical Context

First developed by ancient Greek, Indian, and Arab mathematicians for astronomy and navigation. Hipparchus (c. 190-120 BCE) created the first trigonometric tables.

Key Concepts

Right Triangles

The foundation of basic trigonometry with one 90° angle

Angle Measurement

Degrees (°) and radians (rad) - π rad = 180°

Periodicity

Trig functions repeat every 360° (2π rad)

Triangular Solving

Find missing sides/angles using ratios

Core Trigonometric Functions

Sine (sin)

sin(θ) = Opposite / Hypotenuse

Opposite Hypotenuse θ

Represents the vertical component in the unit circle.

Cosine (cos)

cos(θ) = Adjacent / Hypotenuse

Adjacent Hypotenuse θ

Represents the horizontal component in the unit circle.

Tangent (tan)

tan(θ) = Opposite / Adjacent

Opposite Adjacent θ

Represents the slope of the terminal side in the unit circle.

Reciprocal Functions

Cosecant (csc)

csc(θ) = 1/sin(θ)

Secant (sec)

sec(θ) = 1/cos(θ)

Cotangent (cot)

cot(θ) = 1/tan(θ)

Special Right Triangles

30-60-90 Triangle

Ratio of sides: 1 : √3 : 2. This is a special right triangle where the angle between the legs is 60°, and the other angle is 30°. The hypotenuse is always twice the length of the shorter leg, and the longer leg is √3 times the shorter leg.

45-45-90 Triangle

Ratio of sides: 1 : 1 : √2. This is an isosceles right triangle where both angles are 45°. The two legs are equal in length, and the hypotenuse is √2 times the length of each leg.

Trigonometric Identities

Pythagorean Identities

The Pythagorean identities are fundamental equations in trigonometry derived from the Pythagorean theorem. These identities relate the sine, cosine, and tangent functions to one another.

sin²θ + cos²θ = 1

sin²θ + cos²θ = 1

1 + tan²θ = sec²θ

1 + tan²θ = sec²θ

1 + cot²θ = csc²θ

1 + cot²θ = csc²θ

Angle Sum and Difference

These identities allow us to simplify the trigonometric functions of sums or differences of angles. They are used extensively in solving trigonometric equations.

Unit Circle Trigonometry

Key Properties

The unit circle is a circle with a radius of 1 centered at the origin of the coordinate plane. Trigonometric functions can be defined based on this circle. It plays a fundamental role in connecting algebraic and geometric aspects of trigonometry.

  • Radius = 1 unit
  • Coordinates of any point on the circle: (cosθ, sinθ)
  • Quadrant signs (based on angle θ):
    • Q1: All functions positive (sin, cos, tan)
    • Q2: sin positive, cos and tan negative
    • Q3: tan positive, sin and cos negative
    • Q4: cos positive, sin and tan negative
  • Reference angles: Any angle can be reduced to an equivalent angle in the first quadrant (Q1)

Unit Circle Explanation

The unit circle allows us to easily define trigonometric functions for all angles. The coordinates of a point on the circle are given by (cosθ, sinθ), where θ is the angle formed by the radius with the positive x-axis. This circle is crucial for understanding the periodic nature of trigonometric functions and for finding values of sin, cos, and tan at various angles.

Example: Finding Coordinates of 45° (π/4)

The coordinates of a point at 45° (or π/4) on the unit circle are:

  • cos(45°) = √2/2
  • sin(45°) = √2/2

Real-World Example: Height Measurement

To measure the height of a tree without climbing it:

  1. Measure distance from tree (e.g., 20m)
  2. Measure angle to top (e.g., 40°)
  3. Calculate: height = distance × tan(angle)
  4. Height = 20 × tan(40°) ≈ 16.78m
