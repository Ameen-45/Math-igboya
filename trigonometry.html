Introduction to Trigonometry

Trigonometry is a branch of mathematics that studies relationships between the angles and sides of triangles. Derived from Greek words "trigonon" (triangle) and "metron" (measure), it is fundamental in STEM fields and beyond.

Historical Context

First developed by ancient Greek, Indian, and Arab mathematicians for astronomy and navigation. Hipparchus (c. 190-120 BCE) created the first trigonometric tables.

Key Concepts

Right Triangles

The foundation of basic trigonometry with one 90° angle. These triangles allow us to define the basic trigonometric ratios.

Angle Measurement

Degrees (°) and radians (rad) - π rad = 180°. Radians are often preferred in advanced mathematics.

Periodicity

Trig functions repeat every 360° (2π rad). This periodic nature makes them useful for modeling cyclic phenomena.

Triangular Solving

Find missing sides/angles using ratios. This is the practical application of trigonometric principles.

Core Trigonometric Functions

The three primary trigonometric functions - sine, cosine, and tangent - form the foundation of trigonometry. They relate angles to side ratios in right triangles.

Sine (sin)

sin(θ) = Opposite / Hypotenuse

Opposite Hypotenuse θ

Represents the vertical component in the unit circle. The sine of an angle is the ratio of the length of the side opposite the angle to the length of the hypotenuse.

Cosine (cos)

cos(θ) = Adjacent / Hypotenuse

Adjacent Hypotenuse θ

Represents the horizontal component in the unit circle. The cosine of an angle is the ratio of the length of the adjacent side to the length of the hypotenuse.

Tangent (tan)

tan(θ) = Opposite / Adjacent

Opposite Adjacent θ

Represents the slope of the terminal side in the unit circle. The tangent of an angle is the ratio of the sine to the cosine of that angle.

Reciprocal Functions

Cosecant (csc)

csc(θ) = 1/sin(θ)

The reciprocal of sine

Secant (sec)

sec(θ) = 1/cos(θ)

The reciprocal of cosine

Cotangent (cot)

cot(θ) = 1/tan(θ)

The reciprocal of tangent

Let me explain trigonometric functions differently:

Think of the trigonometric functions as special calculators that tell you about triangle proportions:

  • Sine (sin): Answers "how tall is it compared to the diagonal?"
  • Cosine (cos): Answers "how wide is it compared to the diagonal?"
  • Tangent (tan): Answers "how steep is the slope?"

Memory trick: SOH-CAH-TOA

  • Sine = Opposite / Hypotenuse
  • Cosine = Adjacent / Hypotenuse
  • Tangent = Opposite / Adjacent

These ratios stay constant for a given angle, no matter how big the triangle is. That's what makes trigonometry so powerful!

Special Right Triangles

30-60-90 Triangle

Ratio of sides: 1 : √3 : 2. This is a special right triangle where the angle between the legs is 60°, and the other angle is 30°. The hypotenuse is always twice the length of the shorter leg, and the longer leg is √3 times the shorter leg.

Side ratios: short leg : long leg : hypotenuse = 1 : √3 : 2

45-45-90 Triangle

Ratio of sides: 1 : 1 : √2. This is an isosceles right triangle where both angles are 45°. The two legs are equal in length, and the hypotenuse is √2 times the length of each leg.

Side ratios: leg : leg : hypotenuse = 1 : 1 : √2

The Unit Circle

The unit circle is a fundamental concept in trigonometry that provides a geometric interpretation of trigonometric functions for all angles, not just those in right triangles.

(√3/2, 1/2)

Definition

A circle with radius 1 centered at the origin (0,0) of the coordinate plane. Any point on the unit circle has coordinates (cosθ, sinθ).

x² + y² = 1

Quadrant Signs

  • Q1: All functions positive
  • Q2: Sine positive only
  • Q3: Tangent positive only
  • Q4: Cosine positive only

Remember: "All Students Take Calculus"

Reference Angles

For any angle, the reference angle is the acute angle between the terminal side and the x-axis. This helps find function values for angles in any quadrant.

Reference angle = |θ - nearest x-axis|

Do you understand the unit circle?

The unit circle is essential for understanding trigonometric functions beyond right triangles.

Let me explain the unit circle differently:

Think of the unit circle as a "master key" for trigonometry. It shows you the values of sine and cosine for every possible angle.

How it works:

  • Start at the rightmost point (1, 0) - this is 0°
  • Move counterclockwise around the circle
  • The x-coordinate of any point is cos(θ)
  • The y-coordinate of any point is sin(θ)

Key points to remember:

  • At 0°: (1, 0) → cos=1, sin=0
  • At 90°: (0, 1) → cos=0, sin=1
  • At 180°: (-1, 0) → cos=-1, sin=0
  • At 270°: (0, -1) → cos=0, sin=-1

The unit circle connects geometry (circles) with algebra (coordinates) and trigonometry (functions).

Trigonometric Identities

Trigonometric identities are equations that are true for all values of the variables. They are essential for simplifying expressions and solving trigonometric equations.

Pythagorean Identities

sin²θ + cos²θ = 1

1 + tan²θ = sec²θ

1 + cot²θ = csc²θ

These identities are derived from the Pythagorean theorem and the unit circle equation.

Angle Sum/Difference

sin(A±B) = sinA cosB ± cosA sinB

cos(A±B) = cosA cosB ∓ sinA sinB

tan(A±B) = (tanA ± tanB)/(1 ∓ tanA tanB)

These identities allow us to find function values for sums and differences of angles.

Double Angle

sin(2θ) = 2 sinθ cosθ

cos(2θ) = cos²θ - sin²θ

tan(2θ) = 2 tanθ/(1 - tan²θ)

Useful for simplifying expressions with double angles and solving equations.

Half Angle

sin(θ/2) = ±√[(1 - cosθ)/2]

cos(θ/2) = ±√[(1 + cosθ)/2]

tan(θ/2) = ±√[(1 - cosθ)/(1 + cosθ)]

Helpful for finding exact values of functions for half angles.

Let me explain trigonometric identities differently:

Think of trigonometric identities as "shortcuts" or "rules" that always work in trigonometry, similar to how multiplication rules always work in arithmetic.

The most important identity:

sin²θ + cos²θ = 1

This comes directly from the Pythagorean theorem applied to the unit circle. If you think of a right triangle with hypotenuse 1, then:

  • The horizontal side is cosθ
  • The vertical side is sinθ
  • By Pythagorean theorem: (cosθ)² + (sinθ)² = 1²

Why identities matter:

  • They help simplify complex expressions
  • They allow you to prove other mathematical relationships
  • They provide multiple ways to solve the same problem

You don't need to memorize all identities at once - focus on the most commonly used ones first.

Applications of Trigonometry

Trigonometry has countless real-world applications across various fields. Understanding these applications shows the practical importance of trigonometric concepts.

Engineering & Architecture

  • Structural analysis and design
  • Bridge and building construction
  • Forces calculation in mechanical systems
  • Slope and angle measurements

Example: Calculating the optimal angle for a ramp to meet accessibility standards.

Physics & Astronomy

  • Wave motion and harmonic analysis
  • Projectile motion calculations
  • Celestial navigation and star positions
  • Orbital mechanics

Example: Determining the height of a projectile using its angle of launch and distance traveled.

Navigation & Surveying

  • GPS and satellite positioning
  • Map making and cartography
  • Land surveying and boundary determination
  • Marine and aviation navigation

Example: Using triangulation to determine the distance to an inaccessible point.

Computer Graphics & Gaming

  • 3D modeling and animation
  • Character movement and rotation
  • Lighting and shadow calculations
  • Physics simulations

Example: Calculating the trajectory of a character's jump in a video game.
